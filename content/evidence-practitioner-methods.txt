## Evidence Summary Overview

## Case Study 1: 
### Case Background
- **Organization:** Mayo Clinic
- **Industry/Sector:** Healthcare academic medical center
- **Size/Scope:** Over 75 thousand employees
- **Timeframe:** Initiative began around 2017 and has continued through updates into 2023–2024
- **Source:** Peer reviewed article: https://mayoclinic.elsevierpure.com/en/publications/executive-leadership-and-physician-well-being-nine-organizational/

### Problem Context
- **Problem Description:** Mayo Clinic faced increasing levels of clinician burnout, characterized by emotional exhaustion, reduced professional fulfillment, and rising turnover risk—particularly among physicians and nurses.
- **Problem Severity:** Burnout scores were comparable to or above national averages for physicians. Workload, EHR documentation burden, and staffing shortages were leading contributors.
- **Stakeholders Affected:** Physicians, nurses, advanced practice providers, department leaders
- **Triggering Events:** A series of internal well-being assessments and rising workforce strain revealed systemic issues

### Solution Approach
- **Strategy Chosen:** Their strategy included peer support programs, well-being surveys, and offered leadership training on well-being
- **Implementation Process:** Department based well-being ommittees, Conducted baseline burnout measurement, and Introduced the “Listen-Act-Develop” model for addressing staff feedback
- **Resources Invested:** funding for wellnes intiatives, additional personnel specialed in well-being
- **Timeline:** 18-24 months

### Results and Outcomes
- **Measurable Results:** 
Reduced burnout scores among several clinical departments
Improved engagement metrics in organizational surveys
Increased physician retention
Decreased intentions to leave or reduce clinical hours

- **Qualitative Improvements:** 
Staff reported feeling more supported and heard
Improved teamwork and better relationships with leadership
Greater sense of autonomy and flexibility in scheduling

- **Stakeholder Reactions:** 
Physicians responded positively to reduction in administrative burdens
Nurses found peer support programs especially valuable

- **Unintended Consequences:** 
Resource-intensive programs made smaller departments feel temporarily strained

## Case Study 2: 

### Case Background
- **Organization:** Mountainview Regional Health Network
- **Industry/Sector:** Healthcare system with multiple hospitals/outpatient clinics
- **Size/Scope:** 12,000 employees, 3 community hospitals, and over 50 clinics
- **Timeframe:** 2019–2024
- **Source:**  https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7532952/

### Problem Context
- **Problem Description:** Physicians reported high burnout tied to EHR documentation time, inefficient workflows, and poor work-life boundaries.
- **Problem Severity:** Internal data showed physicians spending up to 2 hours of EHR work outside clinic hours per day (“pajama time”). Primary care turnover was rising.
- **Stakeholders Affected:** Physicians, clinic managers, IT and informatics staff, patients.
- **Triggering Events:** A well-being survey revealed burnout rates above national benchmarks.

### Solution Approach
- **Strategy Chosen:** 
Leadership development program focused on supportive leadership behaviors.
EHR and workflow optimization, including scribes and documentation templates.

- **Implementation Process:** 
Trained department and clinic leaders in evidence-based well-being leadership practices.
Introduced medical scribes in high-volume clinics.
Streamlined EHR templates and automated repetitive data entry.

- **Resources Invested:** 
Budget for scribes and informatics team
Leadership coaching consultants
IT upgrades
Staff time for training and testing EHR changes

- **Timeline:** 
6-8 months for leadership training

### Results and Outcomes
- **Measurable Results:** 

Physician burnout reduced by ~20% over two years.
“Pajama time” decreased by ~45 minutes per day.
Patient throughput increased due to reduced documentation delays.

- **Qualitative Improvements:** 
Physicians felt more supported by leadership.
Better work-life boundaries.
More face-to-face time with patients, less screen time.

- **Stakeholder Reactions:** 
Physicians overwhelmingly supported scribe program.
Some initial budget concerns among administrators.
IT teams appreciated clearer priorities for documentation fixes.
