## Study 1: Workplace interventions to improve well-being and reduce burnout

### Citation
Cohen, C., Pignata, S., Bezak, E., Tie, M., & Childs, J. (2023). Workplace interventions to improve well-being and reduce burnout for nurses, physicians and allied healthcare professionals: A systematic review. BMJ Open, 13, e071203.

### Study Overview
- **Research Design:** Systematic review
- **Population:** Physicians, nurses, and allied health professionals
- **Setting:** Majority os studies conducted in the United states
- **Duration:** Though studies published from 2015 to 2022, the date proves that these interventions still have a major impact on improving well-being.

### Key Findings
#### Related to Your Problem
29 of the 33 studies reported positive outcomes, indicating that workplace interventions can effectively address burnout and well-being.
Burnout reduction was commonly reported, particularly reductions in emotional exhaustion, the primary dimension of burnout.

#### Related to Your Solution
Organizational intervention like Workload reduction, Job crafting, Peer support networks increased compassion satisfaction and significantly reduced emotional exhaustion.

### Relevance Assessment
- **Problem Relevance:** High- The review directly examines burnout and well-being problems among healthcare workers,
- **Solution Relevance:** High- It provides evidence that these interventions do improve well-being and reduce burnout. The review evaluates precisely the types of solutions that have been considered by many organizations

## Study 2: Framework for Reducing burnout in high-stress healthcare settings
### Citation
Holt, K. L. (2025). Enhancing healthcare worker well-being through organizational leadership and policy interventions: A framework for reducing burnout in high-stress healthcare settings. International Journal of Medical and Health Research, 3(3), 7–16. https://doi.org/10.61424/ijmhr.v3.i3.364


### Study Overview
- **Research Design:** Qualitative study
- **Sample Size:** 25 healthcare professionals
- **Population:** Nurses, physicians, clinical supervisors, and mental health specialists working in high-stress healthcare environments, each with at least three years of experience.
- **Setting:** Urban healthcare environments

### Key Findings
#### Related to Your Problem
Lack of support, inconsistent policies, and hostile environments directly increase burnout risk. Workers in ED, ICU, and mental health settings experience emotional exhaustion, psychological strain, and reduced job satisfaction.

#### Related to Your Solution
Leadership has a direct influence on the reducing burnout( positive and empathetic leaders that care for the well-being of their collegeues)
Positive, respectful, collaborative cultures improve mental health, morale, and engagement.
When properly enforced in organizations policies like mandatory breaks, mental health access, flexible scheduling, and debriefing sessions reduce burnout and increase job satisfaction.

#### Important Statistics/Results
Flexible scheduling lead to reduced emotional exhaustion & turnover intention
Mindfulness lead to improved emotional regulation
Peer support lead to higher morale & lower burnout scores
Mental health services lead to reduced anxiety, stigma, and trauma impacts

### Relevance Assessment
- **Problem Relevance:** High - The fous is on ED, ICU, and mental health—some of the most burnout-prone clinical settings.
- **Solution Relevance:** High - since The entire study focuses on organizational, leadership, and policy-level interventions to reduce burnout
- **Context Similarity:** Medium - beacues the study participants are from urban, high-stress clinical environments, highly applicable to most modern healthcare systems.
